# Behavioral Science Reference — CRE Negotiations

## Anchoring Bias

**Source**: Northcraft, G.B. & Neale, M.A. (1987). "Experts, Amateurs, and Real Estate."
Organizational Behavior and Human Decision Processes, 39, 84-97.

**Finding**: Manipulated listing prices anchored property valuations for BOTH amateur
students AND expert real estate agents. The listing price influenced all four dependent
measures: appraisal value, selling price, purchase price, and lowest acceptable offer.

**Critical Detail**: 81% of amateurs listed the listing price as a top-3 consideration.
Only 24% of experts did — yet both groups were equally influenced. Experts denied being
anchored while exhibiting the same magnitude of effect.

**CRE Application**:
1. Always set the first numerical anchor in negotiations
2. Lead with prediction market data as your anchor framework
3. Sellers cannot fully adjust away from your initial reference point
4. Even experienced brokers and appraisers are susceptible

## Loss Aversion

**Source**: Genesove, D. & Mayer, C. (2001). "Loss Aversion and Seller Behavior."
Quarterly Journal of Economics, 116(4), 1233-1260.

**Data**: Boston residential market, 1990-97 boom-bust cycle.

**Findings**:
- Sellers facing nominal losses set asking prices **25-35% above** the difference
  between expected selling price and original purchase price
- They attained higher selling prices of **3-18%** of that difference
- They exhibited a **much lower hazard rate of sale** (sat on market longer)
- Effect was **2x as large for owner-occupants vs. investors** (but held for both)
- Loss aversion behavior explains the positive price-volume correlation in RE markets

**CRE Application**:
1. Identify sellers with basis above current market — expect inflated asks
2. Patience is the primary tool (hazard rate inflection at 90-180 days)
3. Owner-occupied CRE (small biz owners) will be most resistant
4. Offer face-saving structures (owner financing, leaseback, earnback)

## Framing Effects

**Source**: Witte, E.H. (2008). "Framing Effects in Real Estate Negotiations."
Journal of Marketing Research.

**Finding**: Sellers using sales price as a reference point show **greater willingness
to make concessions** than sellers using equity as their reference.

**Harvard PON Addition**: "People are more likely to resist compromises — and even
walk away entirely — when an offer is framed as a loss rather than a gain."

**CRE Application**:
1. Redirect conversation from "what you paid" (equity frame) → "what the market bears" (sales price frame)
2. Frame your offer as a GAIN relative to probability-weighted market outcome
3. Never frame it as a loss relative to their purchase price
4. The realtors/brokers CAN alter these frames — your presentation strategy matters

## Self-Fulfilling Prophecy

**Source**: Diekmann, K.A., Tenbrunsel, A.E., & Galinsky, A.D.
Harvard Program on Negotiation.

**Findings**:
- Negotiators who expected competitive opponents reduced their own demands
- This CREATED the competitive behavior they feared
- Opponents began viewing themselves as more competitive negotiators
- Effect was permanent: "likely never able to be reversed"

**CRE Application**:
1. Present probability-weighted decline analysis → seller internalizes as consensus
2. Seller behavior changes: lower reservation price, more motivation
3. The forecast itself influences the outcome (documented, not speculative)
4. Position yourself as trusted advisor presenting data, not adversary making threats

## Prospect Theory (Foundation)

**Source**: Kahneman, D. & Tversky, A. (1979). "Prospect Theory: An Analysis of
Decision under Risk." Econometrica, 47(2), 263-291.

**Key Findings**:
- Losses hurt ~2x more than equivalent gains feel good
- People underweight probable outcomes vs. certain ones (certainty effect)
- Risk aversion for gains, risk-seeking for losses
- Reference points determine whether outcomes are perceived as gains or losses

**CRE Application**:
1. Frame selling NOW as the certain gain
2. Frame waiting as a probabilistic gamble with negative expected value
3. Losses loom larger — emphasize what they stand to lose by waiting
4. The certainty effect makes your guaranteed close disproportionately attractive
